## Per-block flip-count over-dispersion at 8B q_proj L0

For each 128-block in the tensor, count flips (positions where
sign(W_bonsai) != sign(W_teacher)). Bin blocks by teacher mean(|w|)
into deciles. Compare observed flip-count variance to the Binomial
expectation `128 * p * (1-p)` (the variance under per-element
independent flipping at rate p).

```
decile  mag range          n_blocks  obs_mean  obs_var  exp_var  overdisp
 d 1    [0.002, 0.014]     13108     36.34     71.24    26.02    2.738
 d 2    [0.014, 0.016]     13107     33.94     48.08    24.94    1.928
 d 3    [0.016, 0.017]     13107     33.09     43.75    24.54    1.783
 d 4    [0.017, 0.018]     13107     32.37     41.28    24.18    1.707
 d 5    [0.018, 0.020]     13107     31.38     46.85    23.69    1.978
 d 6    [0.020, 0.022]     13107     31.87     42.99    23.94    1.796
 d 7    [0.022, 0.023]     13107     31.77     33.23    23.88    1.391
 d 8    [0.023, 0.024]     13107     31.26     29.69    23.63    1.256
 d 9    [0.024, 0.025]     13107     30.31     28.55    23.13    1.234
 d10    [0.025, 0.059]     13108     25.51     69.19    20.43    3.388
```

## Reading

- Over-dispersion ratio = observed-variance / Binomial-expected-
  variance.
- Ratio = 1.0: per-element independent flips (Binomial).
- Ratio >> 1: some blocks flip much more, others much less than
  expected — per-block correlation.
- Ratio < 1: regularised / anti-correlated (e.g., enforced quotas).

The observed ratios are 1.2-3.4 — over-dispersed in every decile.
Especially at extremes (d1=2.74, d10=3.39).

**Per-element independent flipping is RULED OUT.** The flip pattern
has block-level correlation: the decision to flip particular
positions within a block is not independent of decisions for other
positions in the same block.

## What this is consistent with

- OBC-style activation-aware optimisation, where each block's signs
  are decided jointly to minimise layer-output error. Block-as-unit
  decisions naturally produce over-dispersion in flip counts.
- A structured LoRA delta with low rank r << 128 — rank-r delta
  affects multiple positions per block coherently, so flips
  correlate within blocks.
- A loss function that rewards block-level coherence (e.g. some
  group lasso variants).

## What this rules out

- Pure i.i.d. Gaussian noise on individual weights with no block
  structure. That would give Binomial flip counts (overdisp ~1.0),
  not the 1.2-3.4 we observe.
- Any per-element-uncorrelated stochastic perturbation.

## Recipe constraint

A reproduction's sign-flip mechanism must produce **block-level
correlated flips**. Pure independent per-element noise on the teacher
will not reproduce the over-dispersion. Mechanisms that DO produce
block-correlated flips include:

1. OBC-style sequential block-wise optimisation against activation
   outputs.
2. Low-rank LoRA structure (rank r affects many block positions
   coherently).
3. Group-aware regularisation in the SGD step.

The bytes don't discriminate among (1)-(3); they only rule out the
i.i.d. baseline.

## Caveats

- One tensor (8B q_proj L0). Per-tensor over-dispersion may vary;
  v_proj might be MORE coherent (consistent with v's lighter
  perturbation), MLP might be LESS coherent.
- Over-dispersion test is sensitive to magnitude-binning. The
  decile choice here uses the Bonsai-8B q_proj L0 distribution;
  finer or coarser bins might shift the numbers slightly but not
  the qualitative finding.

## Gaussian-noise-simulation control

Apply the same over-dispersion test to a Gaussian-noise-simulated
"Bonsai" (`teacher + N(0, sigma=0.025)` then sign-quant) at the same
tensor:

```
decile  obs_mean  obs_var  exp_var  overdisp
 d 1    40.94     32.07    27.85    1.152
 d 2    37.60     25.08    26.55    0.945
 d 3    35.92     23.26    25.84    0.900
 d 4    34.43     23.06    25.17    0.916
 d 5    32.56     22.26    24.28    0.917
 d 6    30.68     21.09    23.33    0.904
 d 7    29.42     20.40    22.66    0.900
 d 8    28.43     19.76    22.11    0.893
 d 9    27.57     19.36    21.63    0.895
 d10    25.07     23.48    20.16    1.165
```

The Gaussian simulation produces over-dispersion of 0.89-1.17 across
all bins — essentially Binomial (variance ratio ~1.0 as expected
under per-element independent flipping).

Bonsai actual: 1.2-3.4 over-dispersion.

**This confirms the Gaussian-noise model is insufficient as a full
model of Bonsai's sign-flip pattern.** The Gaussian model:

- Reproduces the d1-near-0.5 / d10-near-0.0 marginal flip-rate-vs-
  magnitude pattern (force-by-data, see 30_*).
- Does NOT reproduce the within-block correlation structure (this
  test).

The actual mechanism produces flips with block-level correlation
that simple element-wise noise lacks. This is direct byte-level
evidence that the technique includes a block-coherent component:
- could be OBC-style block-wise activation-aware optimisation,
- could be a low-rank LoRA delta with rank << 128,
- could be group-aware regularisation in the SGD step.

The bytes don't discriminate among these mechanism families, but
they DO rule out the pure-i.i.d.-noise baseline.

## Updated implication for the LoRA-noise model from 30_*

The 30_* finding ("Gaussian sigma=0.04 reproduces 1.7B q L0
fingerprint near-exactly") matched FIRST-order statistics
(marginal flip-rate-vs-magnitude). The second-order test (within-
block flip-count variance) fails for the Gaussian model, so:

- The "near-exact" reproduction of the marginal pattern in 30_* is
  necessary but not sufficient for matching Bonsai's full
  fingerprint.
- The implied LoRA delta sigma (~0.025-0.04) is correct in scale
  but the i.i.d. Gaussian shape is incomplete — real LoRA deltas
  ARE block-correlated (rank-r structure), so a low-rank LoRA
  could potentially match the over-dispersion that Gaussian noise
  does not.

A proper reproduction of Bonsai's sign-flip pattern needs a
block-correlated mechanism, not just an isotropic-noise model.
