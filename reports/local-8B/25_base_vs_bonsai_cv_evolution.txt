## Reading

The "MLP-CV-falls-with-depth" pattern (gate/up have wider per-block
scale distribution at early layers and narrower at late layers) is
**inherited from the teacher's natural weight distribution**, not
induced by the technique. Qwen3-8B-base itself has gate/up early-MLP
per-block `mean(|w|)` CV roughly 6x the late-MLP value (0.77 vs 0.13
for gate). Bonsai inherits the same depth shape, just with the values
shifted.

The TECHNIQUE-induced signature, separate from the teacher inheritance,
is:

- For MLP gate/up, Bonsai's per-block CV is roughly HALF the teacher's
  CV at every depth (early gate: teacher 0.77, Bonsai 0.44; late gate:
  teacher 0.13, Bonsai 0.24). The technique COMPRESSES the
  teacher's wide MLP per-block magnitude variation toward a narrower
  distribution. Especially at early layers.
- For attention (q/k/v/o), Bonsai's CV is similar to the teacher's at
  every depth (no compression).
- For down_proj, modest pattern: Bonsai's CV is ~80% of the teacher's
  at every depth.

## What rules out the simple OBC-error-accumulation reading at MLP

Under simple OBC-style sequential error propagation, layer-L+1 should
inherit accumulated activation distortion from layer-L, producing
WIDER per-block scale variation as depth grows (not narrower). But:

- Attention: this prediction holds (CV rises with depth).
- MLP: this prediction is contradicted; CV falls with depth.

The teacher inheritance explains why MLP CV falls (teacher does the
same), so OBC-error-accumulation doesn't necessarily fail — it might
operate at MLP but be MASKED by the teacher's strong opposite trend.
The cleanest way to test OBC at MLP would require running inference,
which we haven't done.

## Recipe constraint

A reproduction targeting Bonsai-style behaviour:

1. Should NOT artificially impose a depth-uniform per-block CV target.
   The natural distribution of mean(|w|) varies by layer (and varies
   per-projection-type), and Bonsai inherits that.
2. Should compress the per-block CV at MLP relative to the teacher,
   especially at early MLP layers. The compression factor is roughly
   2x at early gate/up.
3. Should preserve the teacher's per-block CV at attention without
   compression.

These predictions are not in any of the 9 prior-art papers digested in
`PRIOR_ART_VERDICT_MATRIX.md`. They are 8B-specific findings that
emerged from comparing Bonsai to Qwen3-8B-base directly.

## Refinement from `45_*` (per-layer breakdown of "early MLP")

The "base_early_cv = 0.77 at gate" in the table above is the L0-L3
average. Per-layer the picture is much spikier:

```
L0  gate teacher block_CV  =  0.114   (uniform, like L9-L35)
L1  gate teacher block_CV  =  1.007   (heterogeneous)
L2  gate teacher block_CV  =  1.128   (heterogeneous)
L3  gate teacher block_CV  =  0.834   (heterogeneous)
```

So Qwen3-8B's MLP-gate teacher is uniform at L0 and L9-L35, but
**highly heterogeneous specifically at L1-L3**. The "early MLP CV
high" pattern in 25_* is dominated by L1-L3, not L0. This connects
to the L1-3 over-dispersion spike documented in `40_*` and refined
in `45_*`: the teacher's L1-L3 block-heterogeneity is a precondition
for the per-block flip-count over-dispersion of 10-13× observed
there. Without that teacher structure, no perturbation would
produce such over-dispersion (per `45_*`'s L0-teacher Gaussian
control: rank-128 noise gives over-dispersion ~1.1).

The "compress MLP CV by ~2× at early gate/up" recipe constraint
above is unchanged but should be understood as: Bonsai's recipe
compresses the L1-L3 teacher heterogeneity by a factor of ~2.
At L0 the teacher is already uniform so there's nothing to
compress.
